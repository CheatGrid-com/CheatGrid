# Intelligent Flashcards

Cheat sheets are for *discovering* a topic: scan the whole landscape, from the most-used basics down to advanced patterns, spot related concepts nearby, and get a quick example and description for each. Sometimes that quick look is all you need. When it isn't, flashcards are for *remembering* it, turning those concepts into knowledge that sticks. Each deck is a set of small question-and-answer cards built for one topic, written from a **learning standpoint**: simpler explanations and **concrete examples** that show a specific real case (actual values for a technical idea, a real scenario for a soft skill) rather than an abstract description, so a concept clicks and sticks.

## Why they're "intelligent"

Instead of cramming everything at once, the app shows you a handful of cards and uses a smart schedule to bring each one back **right before you'd forget it**. Every time you flip a card, you tell it how that went:

- **Again:** didn't know it → comes back very soon
- **Hard:** struggled → comes back fairly soon
- **Good:** knew it → comes back later
- **Easy:** instant → comes back much later

Cards you find tricky keep showing up; cards you know drift further into the future. That spacing is what makes studying efficient: you stop wasting time on what you already know. (It runs on **FSRS-6**, the modern spaced-repetition algorithm leading apps use. You just grade each card, and it works out the timing for you.)

## Three ways to study

Open any deck and use the toggle at the top:

- **Review:** your daily "what should I study today." It shows the cards that are **due**, plus a few **new** ones (you choose how many new per day, so a big deck never floods you). Miss a card and it loops back later in the same session until you've got it.
- **Practice:** drill right now, ignoring due dates. Pick your batch **by number of cards** (a set size, or the whole deck) or **by tables** (tick the sections you want to cover). Great before an interview or exam.
- **Browse:** read and search every card, grouped by section. No grading, just reading.

## Core cards first

Many decks tag a **core** subset: the foundational cards worth knowing first. You get to them two ways:

- In **Practice** and **Browse**, the **★ Core only** toggle narrows things to just the core cards, a great place to start a new topic.
- In **Review**, you don't have to pick anything. New cards are introduced **core-first automatically**, so even a fresh session teaches you the essentials before the rest.

## QA Flashcards

Any **[practice test](practice-tests.md)** can also be studied as flashcards: the front is a question, the back is the answer with its explanation. You'll find **QA Flashcards** as a tab inside a Practice Tests topic. They're tracked separately from a topic's regular deck.

## Your progress is saved

When you're signed in, your reviews save automatically in the background. Close the tab any time and pick up exactly where you left off. Leave a session halfway and you'll be offered a **Resume** when you come back.

Studying earns **[XP](xp-and-levels.md)** and keeps your **[streak](streaks.md)** alive, and you can see every deck's progress on your **[Stats](stats.md)** page.

## Free vs. subscription

You get a real taste before subscribing: free decks offer a generous preview, and paid decks let you sample cards in Browse. A **Pro / Pro+** subscription unlocks every deck, every mode, and unlimited study. (Certifications and Soft Skills decks are **Pro+**.)

## Tips

- **A little every day beats cramming.** A daily Review is usually short, which is the whole point.
- **Grade honestly.** Don't always hit "Good"; the schedule works best with your real answers.
- **Before an exam,** switch to **Practice** to hammer a topic, or use **QA Flashcards** on its practice test.
